## Mission Statement

As an educational psychologist with a background in quantitative methods, I got in contact with the emerging open science movement around 2010, when the triplet of the [Bem-controversy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Daryl_Bem#%22Feeling_the_Future%22_controversy), [Stapel-case](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Diederik_Stapel#Scientific_misconduct), and [Ioannidis-paper](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Why_Most_Published_Research_Findings_Are_False#:~:text=%22%20Why%20Most%20Published%20Research%20Findings%20Are%20False,is%20considered%20foundational%20to%20the%20field%20of%20metascience.) got it all started. I enjoy doing open science, thinking about meta-science, and being part of a slow yet daring process towards improvement.

In the spirit of the great thinker and educator John Dewey, I live and teach open science as a process of continuous growth rather than a fixed skill set, defending the position that socially constructed justification is more important than rigid rules and norms. Practices I use most are open data and materials, and I prefer unblinded and transparent reviewing.

I edited journal issues on [registered reports in understudied populations](https://doi.org/10.1027/1015-5759/a000743) and [daring methodologies in Psychology](https://www.frontiersin.org/journals/psychology/articles/10.3389/fpsyg.2023.1091721/full), and have been part of many [analysts](https://escholarship.org/content/qt2jd8c5x8/qt2jd8c5x8_noSplash_11decbeb65d6456e90034854b4f23bd3.pdf)- [endeavors](https://doi.org/10.1080/2153599X.2022.2070255).

For me, open science practices are demanding and require continuous effort and improvement, so I highly appreciate anyone’s engagement.
